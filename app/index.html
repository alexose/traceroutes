Home: Where the Start Is

This is a web app that will show you how data travels across the internet.

To do this, we'll be following the path of the Unix command:

traceroute -I vk.com

Throughout this app, you will see bold blue links. Click these to read more about the highlighted concepts. You can click the red markers to progress through the app.

If you feel like you don't need any further information, and would like to get started right away, please click the marker in the center of the screen (it's just above Brookline). Otherwise, please click here to learn more about traceroute before you start.

West Roxbury

Our traceroute originated in Brookline, so the first stop is the local Comcast hub in West Roxbury. Most consumer Internet Service Providers (ISPs) are Tier 2 providers. This generally means that they operate freely across their own network, but in order to reach other networks, they need to traverse infrastructure that they do not own. They do this by buying IP transit rights or via peering.

Peering refers to the linking of separate networks together so that the users on one network can send traffic across another, and vice versa. This requires the physical exchange of connections via network switches (basically they have to plug in to a common port).

Needham

After West Roxbury, we end up in Needham. We're still on Comcast's network, because that's the consumer-facing option covering much of Massachusetts. At some point, however, we'll need to connect with a different kind of network, known as a Tier 1 ISP.

Put simply, Tier 1 providers can send traffic to all areas of the Internet without paying for access because they own the most wires. They are the ISPs for ISPs.

New York City

Once they make it to New York, Comcast hands our packets off to Level 3, a Tier 1 ISP. Our packets make a few hops in New York, but we're most interested in one of the places it hits before heading across the Atlantic. 60 Hudson Street, in TriBeCa, is a huge facility that acts as the main hub for a huge amount of global internet traffic. From here, our packets will make their way to the fiber optic cable systems that will transport them across the Atlantic Ocean.

Watch this video to learn more about the physical nature of the Internet.

Somewhere in the Ocean

The first transatlantic cables were laid in 1858 for telegraph. Now, our packets travel across any one of a number of different submarine fiber optic cables. We don't have any way of knowing for sure which specific cable our packets traverse, but we do know that Level 3 owns two main transatlantic telecoms cables: Atlantic Crossing 1 (AC-1) and Atlantic Crossing 2 (AC-2). Both operate between New York and the UK, while AC-2 also makes landfall in the Netherlands and Germany.

If you'd like a better sense of what the different transoceanic cable connections look like, here's a map.

London

In London, the London Internet Exchange (LINX) is the UK counterpart to 60 Hudson Street. The response time for our packets between here and this host is just 100-150 milliseconds, despite traveling across thousands of miles of cable. Hundreds of gigabits per second pass through this exchange on their way to locations across the world.

Despite the high throughput these exchanges can handle, they are vulnerable to unexpected spikes in web traffic. Extreme spikes, such as the one that occured in 2013 when hackers targeted anti-spam organization Spamhaus with huge distributed denial of service attacks (DDoS), can cause slowness across multiple regional networks.

Amsterdam

The Amsterdam Internet Exchange (AMS-IX) is one of the largest exchanges in the world. It acts as an exchange point for traffic headed from all over the world to mainland Europe.

Unsurprisingly, the amount of global internet traffic is increasing rapidly. Equally unsurprisingly, the amount of data passing through AMS-IX is also increasing steadily. While there have been concerns about the volume of data overwhelming available infrastructure, thus far these concerns don't appear to be validated by the research.

Dusseldorf

Our packets are still within the Level 3 network. In fact, they'll make it all the way to St. Petersburg in the same infrastructure owned by the same company. For now, the various stakeholders in the global internet exist in an uneasy peace, however the question of who owns and controls the internet will present complex challenges in the near future.

Additionally, issues of censorship, net neutrality, and infrastructural challenges all play a role in the continuing expansion of the Internet in our daily lives. We may, in some dystopic future, have to pay extra for access to sites or services that exist on a different network. For now though, our packets can move on unabated.

Helsinki

Helsinki is our packets' last port of call before Russia. There's nothing particularly special about the internet exchange in Helsinki (apologies to the Finns), but I included this step so I could share this story about an old woman breaking the internet in Armenia by digging a hole in Georgia.

How Does the Internet Work?

Traceroute, ICMPs, and You

Traceroute (on Unix systems) or tracert (Windows) is a tool designed to help analyze the travel path of data across the internet from the originating machine to a target server. The command generates a series of packets and sends them out across the network to the endpoint selected (usually a website). To get to the endpoint, the packets must traverse a series of network nodes, or hosts. At each host, the packets echo information back to the originating machine. This process repeats until the last set of packets reaches its final destination.

In this case, we're send ing an Internet Control Messaging Packet (ICMP) by using the -I flag in our command. The exact science behind an ICMP packet is outside the scope of this application, but for our purposes, it’s enough to understand that an ICMP packet contains the originating Internet Protocol (IP) address of the packet and the destination address. These IP addresses allow each packet to reach its intended destination. From a diagnostic perspective, the most important pieces of information are two timestamps; one set when the packet is sent from the originating server, and one set when it hits a host. Network professionals can use this data to determine if there are any specific nodes in the network that are experiencing high latency. Normal people use traceroute to watch data hop across the world, because it is cool.
